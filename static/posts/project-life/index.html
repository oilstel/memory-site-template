Sunday, December 22, 2024 at 11:10

On Dec 17, 2023 I made a commit to the .git repository projecting.life, a shared container between Gijs de Boer and I. The commit message was simply, “first commit.”

We were in the midst of winter, dreaming about summer days. While winter is sometimes not so fun, I do find the focus and cozy work energy that it brings somewhat enjoyable. Burrowing away and updating your website or reinventing yourself for the spring. There's something about this slower pace that is appealing.

From what I can remember, last winter I was deep into work mode, jumping from project to project, and often getting offered things that didn't align either to my soul or financially or otherwise. While I was grateful for these offers it felt I needed to sets some rules for what I said yes and no to. Often I would join a project only to find that it would lead almost no where. I worked for a company for almost a year, putting my heart into a project only to see it never envisioned due to circumstances out of my control. This really prompted a change. I wanted to find focus and what that looked like was saying no to projects that in my gut didn't align.

Freelance life is so confusing at times. It feels like you are floating down a river and sometimes an opportunity buoy bobs into view and you might grab hold of it until the current pulls it away and you wonder why that buoy was special in the first place what it ever meant.

I talked a lot with Gijs and everyone at Extra Practice about these troubles during this time. These conversations often started or ended in the climbing hall near our studio. I like to think of climbing as an excuse to play for adults. It's like going to the playground with my 30 year old friends yet its socially acceptable. I started to find climbing a grounding activity in my otherwise precarious life.

As the obsession spread, I began watching climbing videos in the evening and while it is totally a waste of time, I did like how seriously these people took something so seemingly meaningless. I came across climbers that would project the same climb over 5-6 years. It was fascinating to watch in the same way I found art strange and fascinating in art school. Why would you make the same painting over 5-10 years? It's seemed on the edge of insanity or at least completely antithetical to societies expectations and that all seemed beautifully radical.

While climbing is more popular than ever right now its interesting to see how it didn't start out that way and how it was very much not this way for a long time. In the same way that computers, the internet, various art forms, all started as these niches. It does feel strange to silo these things. More and more I just think of climbing as another art form.

One thing that keeps me coming back to climbing is this aspect of looking inwardly and using it as a tool for personal growth. There are even climbers trying to capitalize on this aspect, advertising their services as mindset coaches. I think this aspect of working on your self is deeply personal and it would be beautiful to look at it in a more wholesome, less commercial way. Using climbing as a metaphor for life or what we need more of or what we fear, what confidence looks life, how we can test our boundaries.

In the studio one day, Gijs and I began talking about a shared container for this climbing/life research. Could we use climbing as a metaphor for our lives? Could we project a life project in the same way that we project a climb? Could this metaphor give us some distance, breathing space so that we would have a minute to see where we are in on the route (life) and if we are actually enjoying this climb? Would it feel better to do some route finding? Maybe find slightly better feet or try a cross over (something new), and is this route something worth falling for, is it a life project? From my point of view Project Life is a shared container to explore personal growth alongside one another. Another tool in the physiological toolbox. In the same way that art can be used as a therapeutic tool.

We welcome you to explore the first version of this container at projecting.life. Thank you to Benjamin Earl, Emma Verhoeven, Jack Bardwell, Kirsten Spruit for contributing to this project.
